# Lesson 3: Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning - Exercises

## Questions

1. **What are the key differences between single-agent and multi-agent reinforcement learning environments?**
   - Explain how state spaces, action spaces, and reward functions differ when multiple agents are present.

2. **Define the concept of "Nash Equilibrium" in multi-agent game theory. How does it relate to convergence in MARL algorithms?**
   - Provide an example of a game where multiple Nash equilibria exist and discuss the implications for multi-agent learning.

3. **Discuss the difference between cooperative and competitive multi-agent RL settings.**
   - Provide real-world examples of each and explain how reward structures should differ between these two paradigms.

4. **What is the "Credit Assignment Problem" in multi-agent systems, and how does it differ from single-agent settings?**
   - Explain why attributing rewards to individual agents is more challenging in MARL and propose solutions.

5. **Explain the concept of "Communication Protocols" in multi-agent RL.**
   - How can agents learn to communicate effectively? What are the advantages and disadvantages of explicit vs. implicit communication?

6. **Compare centralized versus decentralized training approaches in MARL. What are the trade-offs?**
   - Discuss "Centralized Training, Decentralized Execution" (CTDE) and its benefits for multi-agent learning stability.

7. **What are "Mixed Strategy Equilibria" and how do they apply to stochastic multi-agent environments?**
   - Provide an example where a mixed strategy is the only Nash equilibrium.

8. **Describe QMIX (Factorizing Value Functions for Decentralized Execution) and how it addresses credit assignment in cooperative MARL.**
   - What are the limitations of QMIX and when might other methods be more appropriate?

9. **How does "Population-Based Training" support multi-agent RL, and what are its computational implications?**
   - Discuss how evolving populations of agents enables emergent behavior and competitive dynamics.

10. **Design a multi-agent environment for two competing agents (e.g., game of tag, resource competition).**
    - Specify the state space, action space, reward structure, and predict what equilibrium strategy you expect to emerge.
